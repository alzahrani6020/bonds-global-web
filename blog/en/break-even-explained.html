Break-Even Point Explained Simply: When Does Your Business Start Profiting?

✍️ Bonds Global Team 📅 May 2026 ⏱️ 6 min read
Home / Insights / Profitability & Costs

Most business owners know their sales figures, but very few know their break-even point — that magic number separating loss from profit. If you don't know this number, you're driving your company with your eyes closed.

In this article, we explain the break-even point in the simplest possible way, with examples from real Egyptian and Saudi markets.

What Is the Break-Even Point?

The break-even point is the sales volume (number of units or monetary amount) at which your revenue equals your costs. Before this point, you're losing money. After it, every additional unit puts profit in your pocket.

Break-Even (units) = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price per Unit − Variable Cost per Unit)

Break-Even (amount) = Break-Even (units) × Selling Price per Unit

Practical Example: A Clothing Factory in Egypt

📌 Example

Let's say you own a small factory producing t-shirts:

  • Monthly fixed costs (rent, salaries, electricity): 50,000 EGP
  • Production cost per t-shirt (fabric, sewing, packaging): 80 EGP
  • Selling price to wholesalers: 150 EGP
Contribution Margin = 150 − 80 = 70 EGP
Break-Even = 50,000 ÷ 70 = 715 t-shirts
In monetary terms: 715 × 150 = 107,250 EGP

In other words: you must sell 715 t-shirts per month to cover all your costs. Any quantity below that means a loss. Any quantity above that means profit at a rate of 70 EGP per additional t-shirt.

Practical Example: An E-Commerce Store in Saudi Arabia

📌 Example

Your store sells skincare products:

  • Monthly fixed costs (warehouse rent, subscriptions, salaries): 15,000 SAR
  • Product cost + shipping to customer: 45 SAR
  • Selling price: 120 SAR
Contribution Margin = 120 − 45 = 75 SAR
Break-Even = 15,000 ÷ 75 = 200 products
In monetary terms: 200 × 120 = 24,000 SAR

Why Is the Break-Even Point So Important?

  • You know your minimum volume: Before starting any project, you must ask: "Can the market absorb this volume?"
  • You assess risk: If your break-even point is too high compared to your marketing capacity, the project isn't viable.
  • You make expansion decisions: Before adding an employee or expanding the warehouse, calculate how it affects your break-even point.
  • You negotiate smartly: When you know your margin, you know the lowest price you can accept from a large distributor.

How to Lower Your Break-Even Point

Lowering your break-even point means you start making profit faster. There are three main ways:

1. Reduce Fixed Costs

  • Negotiate rent or move to a smaller space.
  • Use software instead of additional employees in the early stages.
  • Share warehouse or equipment with other companies.

2. Increase Selling Price

  • Add value: better packaging, faster delivery, longer warranty.
  • Target a different customer segment that appreciates value.
  • Don't fear raising prices if the product deserves it — many business owners undervalue their products.

3. Reduce Variable Cost

  • Buy raw materials in larger quantities at lower prices.
  • Optimize production to reduce waste.
  • Negotiate better terms with suppliers.

Conclusion

The break-even point isn't just a mathematical number — it's your decision compass. It tells you when you start making profit, how much you need to sell, and whether your project is viable in the first place. The lower this point, the more flexible you are in the market and the greater your ability to grow.
