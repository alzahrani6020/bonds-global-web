Tax & Zakat for SMEs: What You Must Know Before Year-End

✍️ Bonds Global Team 📅 May 2026 ⏱️ 9 min read
Home / Insights / Tax Compliance

The end of the fiscal year isn't just a date for closing the books — it's a critical deadline for determining whether you've managed your tax and zakat obligations properly. Unfortunately, many small business owners in Egypt and Saudi Arabia make costly mistakes due to insufficient understanding of the system.

This guide covers the situation in Saudi Arabia specifically, with notes on the Egyptian context where details differ.

Income Tax in Saudi Arabia

The Zakat, Tax and Customs Authority (ZATCA) imposes tax on commercial profits at a rate of 20% for foreign companies, and 2.5% zakat for Saudi and GCC shareholders in Saudi companies. With recent updates, the rate is 15% income tax for large companies, while small and medium enterprises benefit from facilitations and reductions.

Who Is Subject to Tax?

  • Commercial companies of all types.
  • Professional and service activities.
  • Capital gains from selling commercial assets.
  • Sole proprietorships whose revenues exceed the exempt threshold.

Zakat: The Fundamental Difference

Zakat is calculated on capital and zakat-eligible assets, not just annual profit. It differs from tax in basis, calculation, and purpose. Saudi and GCC companies are subject to zakat at 2.5% on a specific base (zakat-eligible funds).

📋 Important Note

Mixed companies (Saudis + non-Saudis) are subject to zakat on the Saudi share and tax on the non-Saudi share. This calculation requires high accuracy in records.

Allowable Deductions

You don't pay tax on "gross revenue" — you pay on "net profit." This is where tax deductions become important:

  • Operating costs: raw materials, salaries, rent, electricity, maintenance — provided they are supported by formal invoices.
  • Depreciation: decline in value of fixed assets (equipment, vehicles, furniture) is deductible according to tax tables.
  • Financial expenses: interest on licensed bank loans.
  • Donations: to approved entities up to a certain limit.
  • Previous losses: can be carried forward to offset current year profits.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

⚠️ Mixing Personal and Business Expenses

Using your company account to buy a personal car or household expenses is a tax violation that may result in heavy penalties.

  • Not keeping invoices: Every expense you claim must be supported by a formal invoice. "I remember" is not enough.
  • Late filing: Every day of delay beyond the specified deadline may cost you a percentage penalty on the tax due.
  • Not recording cash revenue: Cash sales must be recorded immediately — hiding revenue is a tax crime.
  • Not understanding the difference between zakat and tax: Filing a zakat return instead of a tax return (or vice versa) causes major problems.
  • Relying on an unlicensed accountant: In Saudi Arabia, the accountant or firm must be registered with the authority.

Filing Deadlines and Compliance

  • Annual tax return: Within 120 days from the company's fiscal year-end.
  • Zakat return: Within 120 days as well, noting that the zakat year may differ from the fiscal year.
  • Advance payments: Some large companies are required to make quarterly advance payments.
  • E-invoicing: Mandatory in Saudi Arabia for most sectors — ensure your system complies with ZATCA requirements.

Egyptian Context: A Quick Overview

In Egypt, the details differ:

  • Corporate income tax ranges between 22.5% and 25% depending on activity and size.
  • Value-added tax is 14% on most goods and services.
  • Tax filing is annual, with mandatory e-invoicing for medium and large companies.
  • There are exemptions for small projects under the Medium, Small and Micro Enterprises Law.

Practical Tips Before Year-End

  1. Review your invoices: Ensure every invoice is entered into your accounting system.
  2. Check inventory: Damaged and obsolete stock must be recorded before closing.
  3. Collect receivables: Bad debts may be deductible if proof conditions are met.
  4. Consult an accountant: At least one month before year-end — don't wait until the last moment.
  5. Calculate estimates: Know your expected tax amount so you can plan for liquidity.

Conclusion

Tax and zakat compliance isn't a punishment — it's a system that protects you from risks and gives you credibility with banks, investors, and customers. Well-organized companies are the ones that can grow and obtain financing. Don't let ignorance cost you more than what you actually owe.
